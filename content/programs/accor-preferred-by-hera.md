# Where *belonging* is quietly, architecturally earned

## An editorial reflection on Accor Preferred by HERA, where travel is stripped of pretense and returned to its architectural and cultural roots.

There is a particular quality to arriving somewhere and feeling, almost immediately, that the place has been expecting you. Not in the performative sense — not the rehearsed greeting or the clipboard at the door — but in the way a room has been chosen with some care, the way a credit appears on your folio without negotiation, the way breakfast is simply there, unhurried, as though the morning itself has been arranged on your behalf. This is the register in which Accor Preferred by HERA operates. Formerly known as Accor STEP, the program has been quietly refined into something more considered — a preferred partner framework that connects our editors, and the travelers we advise, to some of the most architecturally and culturally significant hotel collections in the world. The name HERA carries its own weight: a nod to heritage, to earned standing, to a relationship between traveler and property that is built over time rather than purchased in a single transaction.

The breadth of the portfolio is, in itself, a kind of editorial argument. Orient Express — with its trains, its yacht, and its hotels — represents travel as a form of slow, deliberate culture. Raffles and Fairmont carry the accumulated memory of colonial grandeur transformed into something more self-aware and gracious. FAENA brings a particular Buenos Aires surrealism to the conversation, while Sofitel Legend and the Emblems Collection speak to a European sensibility that prizes patina over polish. Then there are the properties that lean into the contemporary — The Hoxton with its neighborhood-first ethos, 25hours with its irreverent design intelligence, Mondrian and Delano with their mid-century California cool, and SO/ with its fashion-house energy. Banyan Tree, Garrya, and Angasa anchor the program in the wellness and nature-immersed end of the spectrum, while MGallery and Morgan Originals offer the kind of independent-spirited character that resists easy categorization. Together, they form not a monolith but a constellation — each property distinct, each one connected by a shared commitment to the idea that a hotel should mean something.

What Accor Preferred by HERA delivers, in practical terms, is the kind of access that used to require either a long-standing relationship with a concierge or a loyalty tier that took years to accumulate. Through our preferred partnership, guests receive complimentary breakfast for two, a meaningful property credit — typically in the range of one hundred US dollars — priority room upgrades subject to availability, and early check-in and late check-out where the hotel can accommodate. Crucially, all of this sits alongside the best available flexible rate, and every stay continues to earn ALL — Accor Live Limitless — points and night credits as normal. There is no trade-off, no forfeiture of loyalty standing in exchange for the added benefits. It is worth noting, with the transparency our readers expect, that not every property within each brand participates in the program, and some hotels apply specific conditions — a minimum two-night stay, or availability restricted to suite categories. These are not obstacles so much as the natural texture of a program that operates at the level of individual properties rather than as a blanket corporate policy. Our editors are here to navigate exactly that.

The deeper value of a program like HERA is not reducible to a list of amenities. It is about the relationship between a traveler and a place — the sense that one's presence has been noted, that the stay has been considered rather than processed. When we book through our preferred partnership, we are not simply securing a rate; we are establishing a context for the stay. The hotel knows who is arriving, understands the expectations, and has been given the framework to meet them. In a landscape where travel has become increasingly transactional — where the algorithm mediates between desire and destination — there is something quietly radical about a program that insists on the human layer. HERA, in this sense, is less a loyalty scheme and more a philosophy: that the best travel is earned through discernment, and that the finest hotels reveal their character most fully to those who arrive with the right introduction.
